Introduction

I started working on arterial blood flow models as a final year student in the Department of Aeronautics at Imperial College London. My MEng thesis is available here. During my MRes in the Department of Biomedical Engineering, King's College London, we performed a sensitivity analysis on a 1-D model of the human aorta to understand how model parameters influence the estimation of the aortic blood pressure (main results: Figure 1 and Figure 2). My MRes thesis is available here.

My PhD focuses on subject-specific reduced order (0-D and 1-D) computational models of the arterial circulation. Our main aim is the accurate estimation of the aortic blood pressure waveform from magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) combined with non-invasive pressure measurements. As part of this project, we are collaborating with PHILIPS Healthcare to incorporate our model within their prototype clinical MRI software (see MIUA 2016 and AHA 2017).

My most recent work focuses on the identification of optimal haemodynamic parameter estimation methods. To achieve this, 0-D and 1-D blood flow models are run on our nonlinear 1-D finite element framework, Nektar1D. These models are tested on a 1-D virtual (computed) population of healthy subjects which are generated following Willemet's approach. I applied these optimal methods to clinical MRI data to estimate aortic blood pressure in 9 post-coarctation repair patients. This work, presented during a poster session at the 2018 World Congress of Biomechanics in Dublin, is shown here.

I also contribute towards undergraduate education. I have focused on improving the quality of tutorials by thoroughly studying the material to be able to give the students satisfactory answers to their questions, which is rare in current undergraduate education.
